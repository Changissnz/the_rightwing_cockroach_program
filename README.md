# The Right-Wing Cockroach Program

There is a particular species of political animal that thrives not in spite of authoritarian systems, but because of them. Understanding this animal requires understanding the ecosystem that breeds it.

---

## I. The System

When a government stops being a government and becomes a control apparatus, it does not announce the transition. The institutions remain. The titles remain. The procedures, the seals, the oaths — all remain. What changes is the direction of accountability. A government, in its original conception, is accountable downward, to the people. A control apparatus is accountable upward, to its own continuity.

In such a system, the question is never *what is just* but *what is useful*. And what is consistently useful, across centuries and geographies, is a population that polices itself. Not through conscience, but through hierarchy. Every person with a master. Every master with a master above them. The chain descending into the ordinary and the poor, ascending into the invisible and the protected.

The genius of this arrangement is that it requires almost no maintenance once it is running. People enforce their own subordination. They do it at the dinner table. They do it in the classroom. They do it in the family, which is where all political education begins.

---

## II. The Cockroach as Political Category

The cockroach is not merely an insult. It is, in the taxonomy of authoritarian systems, a functional role.

Biologically, cockroaches are territorial, adaptive, and under conditions of scarcity, cannibalistic. They do not organize horizontally. They occupy space and defend it. They survive not by building but by enduring, by outlasting, by consuming whatever is nearest when resources run thin.

The authoritarian state does not create these behaviors in people. It selects for them. It identifies individuals who already operate according to this logic — who hoard, who surveil, who enforce — and it provides them with institutional cover. A small salary, or access to controlled substances, or the silent backing of local law enforcement. Enough to make their behavior feel sanctioned. Enough to make their victims feel that resistance is not only futile but illegal.

This is the Cockroach Program, though it is never called that. It has no official name. It does not appear in any budget. It is simply a pattern, repeated across cities, across demographics, across decades: find the people who will control their immediate environment on behalf of the state, and reward them invisibly for doing so.

---

## III. A Specific and Recurring Case

Consider a type that appears, with remarkable consistency, across immigrant communities throughout the country. The details change. The dynamic does not.

An immigrant mother. She has come from a country she cannot assess clearly, because to assess it clearly would require acknowledging that her society failed her, that the conditions she fled were produced by forces larger than any individual, that her suffering had structural causes and was not simply the result of her own inadequate endurance. This acknowledgment never comes. Instead, the country of origin becomes, in her memory, either a paradise carelessly abandoned or a wound that justifies everything that follows. Either way, the analysis stops there. The question of *why* is never asked. Only the fact of *what happened* is carried forward, as fuel.

She presents to the world as a caretaker. A teacher, perhaps, or a mother, or both simultaneously. These roles require patience, consistency, and the capacity to hold another person's development as more important than one's own comfort. She possesses none of these in adequate supply. What she possesses instead is the appearance of these things, which she deploys selectively, in public, where it counts. At home, behind the door, the appearance is no longer necessary.

At home she is territorial in the way of a creature that has known genuine scarcity and cannot believe the scarcity has ended. The household's resources — financial, spatial, emotional — flow toward her and do not flow back. Family members who cannot or do not contribute financially become targets. Not occasionally. Systematically. The eldest child, in particular, bears this. The eldest is old enough to be useful and young enough to still be trapped. So the eldest is managed through accusation — of laziness, of ingratitude, of drug use, of failure — accusations that keep the eldest perpetually on the defensive, perpetually explaining, perpetually unable to accumulate the stability that would make departure possible.

Here is where the case becomes something beyond a family pathology.

She is, in a number of such cases, not merely a damaged person acting out her damage. She is an instrument. Quietly employed by the local apparatus — the layer of government that sits just below visibility, that interfaces directly with financial institutions, with law enforcement, with the mechanisms by which people's lives are made easier or harder with no formal explanation. The compensation is undisclosed. The arrangement is unwritten. What is exchanged is surveillance for protection. Information for immunity. She reports on the community and in return the community's institutions take her side when disputes arise.

The police arrive and take her side. The courts, when involved, take her side. The social infrastructure, which theoretically exists to protect the vulnerable, recognizes in her the correct symbols — the cross above the door, the flag on the refrigerator, the performance of maternal suffering — and responds accordingly.

And she is, herself, being chemically managed. The same apparatus that employs her supplies her with amphetamines and other destabilizing substances. This is not incidental. A person in a state of chemically-induced hypervigilance is more useful as an informant and an enforcer. She becomes more reactive, more territorial, more prone to the public hysterics that serve the program's purposes — humiliating her targets, drawing official attention to them, marking them within the community as unstable, as problems, as people whose testimony about their own lives should not be credited.

She kicks the eldest out. Calls the police on the eldest. Makes scenes in public places that are calculated, consciously or not, to establish a public record: she is the victim, the eldest is the aggressor. This record will be useful later. It always is.

This is not one woman. This is a recurring figure. She appears in different ethnicities, different cities, different household configurations. What she shares across all instances is the structural position: the state-adjacent enforcer operating at the smallest possible scale, the household, in order to produce the largest possible effect, the atomization of the people most likely to organize against the state if left to develop horizontal solidarities.

## IV. The Immigration Vector

Immigrant communities are particularly fertile ground for this program, for reasons that are structural rather than cultural.

Immigration, in the context of the authoritarian state, is not a humanitarian question. It is a management question. The state allows certain populations in because they are useful — as labor, as demographic ballast, as a constituency that can be threatened with removal and therefore kept compliant. But a compliant population still requires internal management. External policing is expensive and visible. Internal policing is cheap and deniable.

The immigrant who has survived a difficult crossing arrives already conditioned by scarcity. Already familiar with the logic of hierarchy and endurance. Already aware, at a bodily level, that stability is not guaranteed and must be defended. This is not a character flaw. It is the rational residue of irrational circumstances.

The authoritarian state does not heal this conditioning. It weaponizes it.

It finds, within the immigrant community, the individuals whose trauma has curdled into control. Those who mistake domination for safety. Those who, having had nothing, now defend their small territory — a household, a classroom, a block — with a ferocity disproportionate to its size. It offers them affiliation. Not membership in the powerful, but adjacency to it. The feeling of being on the right side of a door that others are locked out of.

In exchange, they report. They destabilize. They break the horizontal solidarities — of family, of neighborhood, of class — that might otherwise cohere into something the state would have to take seriously.

---

## V. The Division of Identity

The program requires that immigrant identity remain fractured along the lines most useful to the state.

This means that different groups must be maintained in different modes of subordination. Black immigrants and Black Americans are maintained in cycles of criminalization — the "barbaric," the "uneducated," the perpetually surveilled and incarcerated, returned again to the same conditions that produce the same behaviors that justify the same surveillance. Hispanic immigrants are kept tribal, associated with narcotics, their political ambitions treated as gang behavior rather than civic participation. Slavic and Asian immigrants are permitted a conditional proximity to the dominant hierarchy, allowed to perform their own cruelties within their own households under the protective canopy of "family values" and "hard work," provided those cruelties do not spill outward into demands on the state.

The white frame absorbs its preferred immigrants — those who perform Christianity loudly enough, patriotism visibly enough — and in doing so gives them permission to act monstrously within their own communities, under the symbols of the cross and the constitution, both of which have been so thoroughly instrumentalized by the state that they now function primarily as clearance badges.

The effect of this division is that the question *what are we doing to each other* is replaced by the question *what are they doing to us*. The horizontal is replaced by the vertical. The class analysis is replaced by the ethnic one. And the state, which produced the conditions for all of it, recedes from view.

---

## VI. The Cannibalism Problem

Every closed system that runs on hierarchy eventually faces the same crisis: the children.

The label "cockroach" was coined to describe Hispanic immigrants specifically. But the behavior it describes — territorial, invasive, cannibalistic under scarcity — is not ethnically bounded. It appears with equal frequency among Slavic families, among Asian families, among any community where the authoritarian transmission of hierarchy from parent to child has been normalized, where the parent's domination of the child is treated as natural order rather than political choice.

The right-wing state respects and reinforces this hierarchy. It intervenes on behalf of the parent. It supplies the parent with institutional backing, with law enforcement, with the social credibility that makes the parent's version of events the official version. The parent who destroys the child is exercising hierarchy. The state understands hierarchy. The state approves.

But when the child refuses — when the child steps outside the transmitted logic, names what was done to them, and refuses to pass it downward to the next generation — the state has a ready response. The child becomes a *rebel*. A *leftist*. A problem case. The diagnosis is political, not psychological, because the threat is political, not psychological. A child who breaks the chain of hierarchical transmission is not a family matter. They are a systemic threat.

The state ensures that this child's credibility is preemptively destroyed. Every institution that child might appeal to — the school, the police, the court, the social service — has already received, through the accumulated record of the parent's public performances and official reports, a version of events in which the child is the unstable one. The child arrives at every institution already labeled. Already guilty. Already the aggressor in a theater where they were always cast as the aggressor, regardless of what actually occurred behind the door.

The child who cannibalizes the program by simply surviving it and describing it is treated as the cannibal.

---

## VII. What Remains

The program does not require monsters, though it produces them. It requires only a system that rewards certain behaviors and ignores the cost of those rewards. It requires institutions willing to look the other way in exchange for order. It requires a definition of order that means *the hierarchy is stable* rather than *the people are well*.

And it requires, above all, the constant obscuring of a simple fact: that the cockroach enforcing the hierarchy at the household level is not the enemy of the person beneath them. They are the instrument of the person above. The cruelty moving through the household, the classroom, the neighborhood, does not originate there. It arrives there, having traveled a long distance through clean corridors and well-lit offices, before it lands on the most exposed.

The question the program cannot survive is not *who is hurting me* but *who benefits from my being hurt*. That question, followed honestly, does not end at the kitchen table. It does not end at the precinct. It ends somewhere much further up, in rooms where the question itself is considered the most dangerous thing a person can ask.

The program's final protection is the convincing of the person at the bottom that they arrived there through personal failure. That the hierarchy is natural. That the parent was simply being a parent. That the suffering was a family matter, private, unconnected to anything structural or political, and therefore no one else's concern, and therefore no one else's responsibility to change.

This conviction, installed early and reinforced constantly, is the program's masterpiece. It does not need walls or guards. It lives in the person who carries it. It is passed down, like a name, like a face, from one generation to the next — until someone, at great personal cost, refuses to pass it on.
